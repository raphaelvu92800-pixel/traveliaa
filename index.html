Destination Guide

Bali Beyond the Clichés

A Luxury Family Guide for 2025 · Deva Xavier · 8 min read

Bali has long suffered from its own success. The image of a Kuta beach, crowded and chaotic, has become shorthand for everything mass tourism represents. But step away from the southern coast and a different island reveals itself — one of quiet rice terraces, family-run warungs, and luxury that feels earned rather than imposed.

Where to Stay: Beyond the Resort Compound

The most discerning families are increasingly choosing private villas in Ubud or Canggu over traditional resort stays. A four-bedroom villa with a private chef, infinity pool overlooking the jungle, and a dedicated housekeeper costs a fraction of a comparable hotel suite — and offers infinitely more freedom for children to move, play, and explore.

The Temples: A Ritual, Not a Tourist Stop

Visiting Tanah Lot or Uluwatu at sunrise, before the tour buses arrive, is a profoundly different experience. Arrange a private guide through your villa and treat it as a cultural immersion, not a photo opportunity. Children aged 6 and above respond remarkably well to the ceremonies if contextualized properly.

Travelia Tip

Book your Bali villa at least 3 months in advance for peak season (July–August, December–January). Travelia clients receive priority access to pre-vetted properties not listed publicly.

Eating Well: From Warungs to World-Class

Bali's food scene has undergone a quiet revolution. Locavore in Ubud consistently ranks among Asia's best restaurants; Sardine in Seminyak offers extraordinary Balinese cuisine in a rice paddy setting. For families with children, the half-dozen excellent beach clubs around Canggu offer quality food, pools, and a relaxed atmosphere that works for all ages simultaneously.

Japan with Kids

The Ultimate 10-Day Cultural Immersion · Marc Rivière · 12 min read

Japan is, counterintuitively, one of the best countries in the world to travel with children. The culture's respect for order, its obsession with quality at every price point, and its extraordinary public transport system make logistics effortless. What's harder is knowing which experiences genuinely work for mixed-age families.

Tokyo: Structure Your Days Around Energy Levels

Begin with Shibuya and Harajuku for the teenagers; dedicate an afternoon to Akihabara (which children of all ages adore); save Senso-ji and Ueno for the morning when everyone is fresh. The secret weapon: teamLab Planets, an immersive digital art museum that works for ages 3 to 93.

Kyoto: Slow Down and Let It Settle

Two nights minimum in a ryokan is non-negotiable. The ritual of bathing, the kaiseki dinner, the sleeping on tatami — this is the experience children remember for decades. Book a private tea ceremony for the family; it's surprisingly accessible and the host will adapt the formality to your children's age.

Travelia Tip

The JR Pass pays for itself if you travel between Tokyo, Kyoto and Osaka. Book it from France before departure — it cannot be purchased at the same price in Japan.

Tuscany's Hidden Estates

Private Villas, Michelin Stars & Sunset Drives · Alexandra Levi · 10 min read

The Chianti hills between Florence and Siena have inspired artists, writers and wanderers for centuries. What's changed recently is the emergence of a generation of estate owners who've converted historic farmhouses into extraordinary private residences — available by the week to discerning families seeking space, privacy and authenticity.

The Estate Experience: Why It Beats Any Hotel

A 6-bedroom Chianti estate with pool, vineyard access, private chef and daily housekeeping offers an intimacy impossible in any hotel. Children explore freely; adults have genuine privacy; and everyone gathers for dinner as if the house were always theirs. The cost, divided across a large family, is often comparable to two standard hotel rooms in Florence.

The Culinary Dimension

Tuscany rewards food obsessives. Book a table at Osteria Francescana in Modena (plan 6 months ahead) or La Tenda Rossa near San Casciano. For more accessible excellence, the agritourism restaurants dotting the Chianti countryside consistently offer extraordinary pasta, local cheeses, and wines that cost a fraction of what they'd fetch in London or Paris.

Travelia Tip

September is the ideal month: the harvest is underway, temperatures are perfect for exploring, and the summer crowds have thinned. Travelia can arrange private winery tours with access usually reserved for trade buyers.
